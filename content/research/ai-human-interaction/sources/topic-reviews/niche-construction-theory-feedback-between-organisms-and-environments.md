# Niche Construction Theory: Feedback Between Organisms and Environments


Figure: In the standard evolutionary model (A), the environment imposes selection on organisms with no feedback. The niche-construction model (B) adds organism-driven environmental change (red arrows) and ecological inheritance, creating a feedback loop between organism (gene pool) and environment[1]. Niche-construction theory (NCT) posits that organisms actively modify their environments, and these modifications in turn alter the natural selection pressures on themselves and others[2]. In effect, organisms are not just passive subjects of fixed environmental forces, but co-constructors of their niches[2]. Classic examples include beavers building dams or earthworms burrowing soil – such activities change moisture, nutrients, and community structure, directly affecting the survival and reproduction of descendants[2][3]. When these niche changes persist across generations, they create an ecological inheritance – offspring inherit a modified environment built by ancestors[4]. NCT thereby emphasizes feedback loops as the unit of analysis: adaptations emerge from a circular process in which organismal activities alter selection pressures, which then act on organismal traits in the next generation[2][1]. In short, evolution is driven not only by genes and selection, but by the dynamic interplay of organisms and the environments they construct[2][1].

Organismal niche construction: By behavior, metabolism or technology, an organism changes its local environment. For instance, “organisms actively change their own environment” – e.g. earthworms leaving behind a moist mucus layer that lets them survive on land[3]. Such niche construction systematically alters the selection pressures the population experiences[2].

Feedback and ecological inheritance: When environmental modifications are stable (e.g. a built nest or cultured microbiome), offspring inherit a changed niche. This ecological inheritance means a trait’s fitness effect depends on predecessors’ niche construction[4][1].

Loop as unit of analysis: NCT explicitly contrasts with the unidirectional model. In the niche-construction perspective, organisms ↔ environment form a feedback loop (Fig 1B): the organism’s own gene pool both affects, and is affected by, the modified environment[1]. Adaptation thus cannot be understood without modeling this coupled loop[1].

# Extended Evolutionary Synthesis and Debates

Within the Extended Evolutionary Synthesis (EES), niche construction is elevated to a fundamental evolutionary process alongside natural selection, genetic drift, etc. EES advocates (Laland, Feldman, Odling-Smee and others) argue that including niche construction – plus related factors like epigenetic and cultural inheritance – changes how we model adaptation. For example, Laland et al. define niche construction broadly as organisms’ “metabolism, activities and choices” that “modify or stabilize environmental states” and thus alter selection on themselves and other species[5]. They show formally that niche-constructing actions can shift equilibria, change evolutionary rates, and even allow traits normally deleterious to spread[5]. From the EES viewpoint, evolution is inherently reciprocal: adaptation is co-produced by genes, development and environmental change, blurring the boundary between organism and environment[5][6]. For instance, the EES “offers a developmental view of ecology” focusing on organisms’ agency and their construction of niches that are then inherited by descendants[6]. In this way niche construction connects “selection-focused (Darwinian)” and “development-focused (Lamarckian)” factors in a unified framework[7].

Critiques: Not all evolutionary biologists accept that niche construction requires revising theory. Critics (e.g. Scott-Phillips et al. 2014, Wray et al. 2014) argue that standard evolutionary models already account for organisms altering their environments, and that these effects do not constitute a separate “process” equal to selection[8][7]. In an adversarial-collaboration paper, Scott-Phillips et al. note that proponents see niche construction as profoundly changing evolution, whereas skeptics see it as a convenient but non-fundamental way to describe how organisms affect their own selective regimes[8]. Wray and colleagues similarly contend that the phenomena (soft inheritance, developmental bias, niche construction) are interesting but not novel – they do not necessitate rewriting the Modern Synthesis[7]. In short, the debate hinges on whether niche construction is simply the expected interplay of behavior and environment, or a concept that forces us to “rethink” evolutionary causation[8][7].

# Niche Construction in Culture and Technology

Niche-construction concepts have been applied to human culture, technology and cognition. For example, Kim Sterelny’s The Evolved Apprentice (2012) argues that human cultural evolution depended on informational engineering by older generations. Sterelny observes that humans “organise the learning world of the next generation. Humans (like many other organisms) modify their own environment. One important form of human niche construction is informational engineering”[9]. In practice, this means parents and mentors create structured learning niches (tools, artifacts, tasks) that scaffold child development and skill transmission across generations[9]. Such apprenticeship-learning models illustrate a cultural niche feedback: as one generation’s technology and practices improve, they create a richer environment in which the next generation learns, leading to cumulative cultural innovations[9].

Similarly, Cecilia Heyes’s Cognitive Gadgets (2018) treats uniquely human cognitive traits (language, imitation, mindreading) as products of cultural niche construction. Heyes shows how cultural practices reshape biology: for instance, the advent of agriculture, food storage and modern diet changed human physiology. As one reviewer notes, “cultural practices such as storing seasonal surplus, agriculture, and modern food production affect phenotypic traits such as body size, cognitive development, and gut bacteria[10].” In Heyes’s view, cultural inventions become part of the developmental niche for each new generation, canalizing how human minds develop. In short, these cases treat tools, norms and institutions as niche-constructing factors that feed back on our evolution[9][10].

Cultural learning: Humans create and inherit knowledge-rich niches. Sterelny highlights “informational engineering” – older generations tailoring environments (tools, games, institutions) so children learn effectively[9].

Cognitive gadgets: Heyes proposes human “gadgets” (cognitive capacities) shaped by culture. Niche construction is seen in how literacy, farming, etc., alter selection on the brain and body[10].

Extended minds: Analogous ideas appear in extended cognition research: tools and media become part of our cognitive niche. For instance, Andreas Högberg (2026) emphasizes an evolving “socio-technical niche” co-shaped by technology and human brains[11]. Each technological medium (from stone tools to AI) continuously reshapes our cognitive environment, and thus our own mind’s evolution[11][9].

# AI as a Niche-Constructing Technology

Viewed through the niche-construction lens, AI systems are agents that modify human environments (cognitive, informational, technological) and thus co-evolve with us. Recent work explicitly frames human–AI interaction as reciprocal:

Feedback loops in AI systems: Pedreschi et al. (2024/2025) introduce “human-AI coevolution”, noting that recommender systems and AI assistants create a continuous loop: users’ choices generate data that train AI models, which then influence future user choices[12][13]. In other words, users and AI systems are in a perpetual feedback loop rather than a one-way tool use.

Cognitive niche extension: Frontiers in Psychology (Högberg 2025) argues that our “socio-technical niche” co-evolves with cognition[11]. AI is simply the latest iteration of this process: as we build and integrate AI into daily life, our cognitive environment (information flows, decision aids, social context) is reshaped in real time. Technologies become embedded ‘extensions’ of mind, altering attention, memory and decision-making[11][9].

Model collapse & polluted data: A critical issue is that AI changes its own training environment. Shumailov et al. (2024) formalize “model collapse”: generative AI trained on data including its own previous outputs will gradually forget the true data distribution, especially rare signals[14]. This self-reinforcing loop effectively narrows the AI’s output over iterations. Cito & Bork (2025) dub this the “polluted well” in software: LLM-generated code (often containing subtle bugs or biases) gets committed to public repositories, then used as training data for the next generation of models[15][16]. Over time this can cause a code collapse: unique or optimized solutions (the “tails” of the distribution) are lost, and ecosystems converge on bland, vulnerable patterns[15][17]. They warn that “replacing the human engineer caps the intelligence of the software ecosystem at the level of the current model… turn[ing] engineering into a closed loop”[16]. In short, AI can inadvertently engineer its own niche into one that favors its biases (and amplifies human biases).

Bias amplification: Empirical work confirms pernicious feedback. For example, Glickman & Sharot (2024/25) show that when humans interact with an AI that has been trained on their biased judgments, the AI amplifies those biases, and subsequent human responses become even more biased[18]. This is a clear niche-like feedback: a small initial human bias is magnified by AI and then “inherited” by other humans, further entrenching the bias[18].

Epistemic niche construction: Scholars (e.g. Bodong Chen et al., 2026) are beginning to use the term epistemic niche construction to describe how humans use AI to build and modify their shared knowledge environment (tools, data practices, analytic norms). Although still emerging, this idea treats AI as reshaping our collective “knowledge niche” in ways that feed back on learning and discovery.

Together, these perspectives treat AI not as a passive tool, but as an environment-modifying agent with recursive effects on human cognition, data flows, and design spaces[12][15]. The AI-driven niches we create (e.g. code repositories filled with AI-suggested code, media feeds curated by algorithms) loop back to shape future AI performance and human adaptation.

# Methodological Implications: Studying Human–AI Niche Coevolution

Adopting a niche-construction view of human–AI systems demands new research methods. Instead of evaluating a “tool–user dyad” in isolation, researchers must model the coevolving loop: humans shape the AI environment (e.g. by generating or labeling data), and that environment in turn reshapes humans and future AI behaviors. Practical implications include:

System-level and longitudinal studies: One must study entire socio-technical ecosystems over time. For example, analyze how open-source codebases evolve before and after deploying an AI coding assistant. Track metrics like code diversity, error rates, and innovation (akin to biodiversity measures) rather than only immediate productivity. Cito & Bork’s findings suggest watching for narrowing patterns as signals of collapse[17]. Similarly, monitor recommendation networks or social media traces to detect feedback amplification. Pedreschi et al. call for techniques that explicitly capture feedback-loop mechanisms[13] – for instance, time-series analyses of user preferences under shifting algorithmic suggestions, or network models of information flow.

Experimental co-design: Laboratory and field experiments should embed iterative AI interactions. For instance, an A/B test might compare developers solving tasks with vs. without AI assistance over several coding tasks, measuring not just speed but how the code produced in each round influences the next round of AI suggestions. Glickman & Sharot’s design – alternating human judgments with AI-provided judgments – is one model of a controlled feedback-loop experiment[18]. In creative domains, one could simulate an “AI ecosystem” where participants iteratively refine content with and without seeing AI outputs, tracking how quickly novelty degrades. Agent-based simulation is another approach: model populations of AI agents and human agents exchanging information, to predict long-term dynamics under different rules (e.g. rate of human vs. AI input, diversity of sources).

Metric development: New metrics are needed to quantify niche-construction effects. Traditional measures (accuracy, click-through, lines-of-code) are insufficient; one should measure environmental change. Examples: the variance or entropy of an AI-generated code corpus over time, the rate of emergence/disappearance of novel code idioms, or the magnitude of human performance bias shifts after repeated AI interaction (as in the bias amplification study). We can adapt ecological concepts: e.g. an “effective population size” of ideas, or a “selection pressure” index derived from how much environments (code or data) differ from generation to generation. Tracking these requires instrumentation: e.g. mining version-control histories to see how often AI-generated contributions are modified by humans.

Interdisciplinary approaches: These research designs combine psychology, HCI, and complex-systems methods. Mixed methods will be key: quantitative analytics (big-data logging, simulation modeling) plus qualitative/ethnographic work (interviews on how developers perceive AI influence, observations of workplace AI adoption). In software engineering specifically, one might conduct multi-year ethnographies in organizations that adopt AI coding tools, examining how team practices, architectural decisions, and developer skills co-develop with AI use.

Control vs. feedback: Most existing AI evaluations control for feedback (training sets are fixed). A niche-construction approach requires dynamic data: for example, iterative retraining cycles or “closed loop” scenarios where the AI sees its own output. This means experimental designs must explicitly include the contamination step: e.g. retrain a model on code that includes or excludes its previous outputs, then compare performance and diversity, as Shumailov et al. do in simulation[14].

In sum, research on AI-augmented environments should be coevolutionary: define hypotheses about looped influence (e.g. “AI overuse will reduce solution diversity”) and test them via models and empirical studies that span multiple cycles. Pedreschi et al. suggest this requires rethinking methodology itself, borrowing tools from complexity science and network theory to capture the feedback loop[13]. In practice, any study of AI effects must consider downstream consequences on data and human behavior, not just immediate task outcomes.

# Research Program: Coevolving Human–AI Work Environments

A coherent research program would treat AI-augmented workplaces (especially software engineering) as coevolving eco-social niches. Key components would include:

Computational modeling: Develop formal models of coevolution. For software, create agent-based simulations where “developer” agents and “AI” agents exchange code, to explore scenarios of collapse or stable diversity. For social media/knowledge systems, model how content generation by AI and humans co-propagate. These models should incorporate niche-construction parameters (rate of environmental modification, fidelity of inheritance, etc.) and be used to predict regimes of stability vs. degeneration[13].

Empirical longitudinal studies: Collect and analyze real-world data streams. In software ecosystems, this means longitudinal analysis of code repositories as AI tools (e.g. GitHub Copilot) are adopted. Key data include commit histories, test failure rates, bug incidence, code similarity metrics, and community behavior (e.g. reliance on AI suggestions over time). In organizational settings, measure developer skill development, error rates, and decision patterns before vs. after AI introduction. For cognitive niches, one could study how patterns of information search or problem-solving evolve when AIs become common collaborators.

Experimental feedback-loop tests: Design controlled experiments where human participants iteratively use and train AI systems. For example, have participants alternately write and review AI-generated code for several rounds, or alternate making and judging decisions with AI guidance. Measure changes over rounds in creativity, error rates, biases or other outcomes[18]. Similarly, pedagogical experiments could test learning with vs. without AI-enhanced environments, observing how knowledge retention and transfer differ when learners are embedded in AI-modified “niches”.

Metrics for “niche health”: Develop and validate quantitative measures of environmental change. In software, this could include entropy of function usage, dispersion of programming styles, or prevalence of certain security vulnerabilities over time. In more general AI systems, metrics like data diversity, algorithmic bias indices, or knowledge novelty scores could serve. Link these metrics to niche-construction theory (e.g. treat divergence from baseline as measure of ecological inheritance strength) and test for correlations with system performance or user outcomes.

Intervention and co-design studies: Investigate how deliberate niche management can shape coevolution. For example, implement policies like “human-in-the-loop” gates (as suggested by Cito & Bork[19]) and measure their effect on the loop. Experiment with hybrid AI–human design processes: teams design software with some members using AI suggestions and others using only human-generated code, to see how the resulting ecosystems differ. The goal is to identify practices that maintain beneficial diversity and prevent runaway collapse, effectively engineering the niche rather than letting it evolve unchecked.

Theory integration: Continuously refine theoretical frameworks by linking findings back to evolutionary concepts. For instance, if code collapse is observed, map it onto the notion of niche stasis; if rapid innovation occurs, identify the niche-construction triggers. Engage evolutionary theorists to incorporate digital ecosystems into extended synthesis models. This can yield general principles (e.g. “AI as anthropogenic niche construction”) and guide future AI design (e.g. designing AI that introduces “controlled variability” to prevent collapse).

By treating human–AI systems as coevolving niches, this research program would break down silos between AI, cognitive science, and evolutionary theory. It would produce both deep theory (new models of socio-technical evolution) and practical guidance (design strategies to sustain healthy AI–human ecosystems). For example, insights from such research might inform software engineering education (ensuring developers retain “cognitive muscle” by deliberately alternating AI use) and data governance (maintaining “clean” human-generated training sets) as Cito & Bork suggest[19]. In sum, a niche-construction approach to AI-augmented work aims to consciously engineer the evolutionary loop – harnessing feedback for positive adaptation, while guarding against deleterious collapse of our digital niches.

# 2) Synthesis (What Holds Across Studies)

Across these studies, a consistent structure emerges:

### 1. The Loop Is Real and Measurable

Multiple domains (bias judgment, recommender systems, generative modeling, software engineering) show bidirectional influence: 

Humans → data → AI → humans 

This aligns tightly with niche construction’s organism ↔ environment feedback loop. 

### 2. The Direction of the Loop Is Not Neutral

Across empirical and simulation evidence:

Bias → amplified (Glickman & Sharot) 

Data → degraded (Shumailov) 

Code → homogenized (Cito & Bork) 

👉 The loop tends toward:

variance reduction 

tail loss 

path dependence 

This is exactly what niche construction predicts when:

environmental modification is high-fidelity 

variation is not replenished 



### 3. Ecological Inheritance Has a Direct Analog

In biology:

organisms inherit modified environments 

In AI systems:

models inherit data environments shaped by prior models 

Examples:

Training sets polluted with AI outputs (model collapse) 

Codebases shaped by AI suggestions (polluted well) 

Recommendation logs shaping future preference landscapes 

👉 This is digital ecological inheritance



### 4. The Unit of Analysis Must Shift

All credible studies implicitly reject:

“human uses tool → outcome”

Instead, they operate on:

recursive system dynamics over time

This is the strongest convergence point across:

complexity science 

AI safety (model collapse) 

behavioral experiments (bias loops)
