# Transactive Memory in Human-AI Teams

## Scope and evidence standards

This scan prioritizes primary sources and separates three evidence bands: the classic transactive-memory literature; the still-small set of studies that directly apply or adapt transactive-memory ideas to human-AI teaming; and adjacent studies of AI-supported coding work that do not measure transactive memory systems directly but do illuminate the same underlying dynamics of specialization, reliance, coordination, and handoff. Across the post-2020 literature, direct TMS-for-HAI evidence is still sparse. ## Direct empirical evidence on TMS in human-AI teams

The strongest direct application is the 2023 ICU simulation study. It observed 180 physicians and nurses in human-AI teams and coded transactive-memory-related behaviors with TRAWIS, adding AI-specific observation categories. The main result is not that ÔÇťAI improves TMSÔÇŁ in general. It is more conditional: accessing knowledge from the AI was positively linked to novel hypothesis generation and speaking-up behavior, but only in higher-performing teams; accessing knowledge from human teammates was negatively associated with those behaviors regardless of team performance. That is best read as evidence that AI can function as a knowledge source inside a transactive system, but only when the broader team process is already functioning well. It is also important that the measure was behavioral rather than a validated revised HAI TMS scale. ţłÇciteţłéturn4search1ţłéturn27search5ţłü

The 2021 embodied-conversational-agent study is relevant but narrower. It asks whether an agentic leader can affect perceived TMS, not whether an AI teammate becomes a full participant in a distributed memory system. Both human and agent leaders were perceived as potentially improving team TMS, but the design is an online perceptual study and the outcome is perceived TMS rather than observed memory performance. That makes it useful for interface and leadership cues, but not sufficient as evidence that HAI teams have developed a stable TMS in the classical sense. ţłÇciteţłéturn8search0ţłéturn8search3ţłü

The 2023 COHUMAIN paper and the 2025 TIMS paper are more theory-building than measurement-building. COHUMAIN empirically tests a TMS-related model in 78 human teams and then extends it to HAI through nine expert interviews. The TIMS study is an abductive qualitative case study arguing that intelligent agents can be treated either as external memory supports or as part of a joint transactive memory system depending on task complexity, knowledge intensity, and the agentÔÇÖs task competence. Both are important because they move beyond simple ÔÇťAI-as-toolÔÇŁ language, but neither gives the field a validated HAI TMS instrument. ţłÇciteţłéturn32search2ţłéturn34view0ţłü

The unequal-access experiment is not framed as TMS research, but it is one of the most revealing adjacent studies. Sixty two-person teams were assigned to unequal, full, or no AI access. Unequal access produced the best productivity, with better task quality and faster completion than either full or no access. The reported mechanism is also TMS-like: more concentrated questioning and more cognitive diversity. In plain terms, not everyone needs equal access to the AI for a distributed knowledge system to improve; in some conditions, asymmetry may sharpen specialization. ţłÇciteţłéturn16search3ţłéturn16search2ţłü

## Synthesis

The literature converges on a simple but important point: classic TMS is a theory of distributed knowing, not outsourced storage. The original work and later team-cognition research show that teams perform better when expertise is differentiated, members learn a reliable map of who knows what, and retrieval is coordinated through repeated interaction and grounded communication. The direct HAI literature does not yet demonstrate that current AI teammates routinely produce that full package. Instead, it shows something narrower. Teams can sometimes use AI as a high-value knowledge source; asymmetries in AI access can reshape specialization and questioning in productive ways; and coding teams increasingly redistribute work so that humans do less first-draft production and more planning, prompting, reviewing, debugging, and integration. That is TMS-like in structure, but not equivalent to mature human TMS. The abundant memory store is not the hard part. The hard part is calibrated reliance, stable retrieval routines, and mutually understood competence boundaries. ţłÇciteţłéturn2search5ţłéturn26search7ţłéturn29search0ţłéturn4search1ţłéturn16search3ţłéturn24search0ţłéturn15search3ţłü

A second synthesis point is that shared history still matters. Classic TMS research repeatedly shows that familiarity, face-to-face interaction, and richer cues help teams develop reliable meta-knowledge and retrieval routines. HAI systems typically do not come with this shared embodied history, and model versions, interfaces, context limits, and policies can change underneath the team. That means an HAI TMS may be more fragile over time than a human-only TMS unless the organization deliberately stabilizes interfaces, records interaction history, and trains teams in when to delegate, when to inspect, and when to override. The recent measurement literature strengthens this interpretation by showing that TMS effect sizes look larger in self-report than in observer or embedded measures. In HAI settings, where rich traces are available, the field should resist the temptation to call any positive AI attitude a TMS and should instead measure actual retrieval and coordination behavior. ţłÇciteţłéturn26search0ţłéturn26search7ţłéturn29search0ţłéturn28view0ţłéturn31view0ţłü

## What a credible TMS-for-HAI panel study would need to measure

A credible 6-to-24-month panel study should treat TMS-for-HAI as a multi-method, evolving team property rather than a one-time survey score. At minimum, it would need to measure the following.

First, it would need **separate maps of expertise location**: human-to-human, human-to-AI, and AI-to-human task routing. In practical terms, that means repeated measures of who the team believes is best for what kind of problem, who actually gets consulted, and whether those maps converge or drift over time. A revised Lewis-style scale could do part of this work, but it would need AI-specific items rather than simply swapping ÔÇťteammateÔÇŁ for ÔÇťAI.ÔÇŁ ţłÇciteţłéturn27search8ţłéturn4search1ţłü

Second, it would need **behavioral measures of retrieval and coordination**, not just attitudes. The ICU studyÔÇÖs use of TRAWIS points in the right direction, and the newer text-based TMS measure shows that communication traces can be mined for specialization, credibility, and coordination markers. For HAI coding teams, that implies instrumenting chat logs, IDE prompts, code comments, pull requests, review threads, and explicit escalations between human and agent. ţłÇciteţłéturn27search5ţłéturn31view0ţłü

Third, it would need **calibration and reliance measures**. Classical credibility becomes calibration in HAI: acceptance rate of AI suggestions, revision depth after acceptance, time spent verifying, frequency of overrides, instances of blind adoption, and downstream defects or rework. The coding literature shows why this is essential: the same tool can speed up bounded tasks, help some teams, and slow down expert work in familiar codebases because verification costs differ dramatically by context. ţłÇciteţłéturn11search5ţłéturn24search0ţłéturn15search3ţłü

Fourth, it would need **handoff and specialization metrics**. A credible panel should record who initiates delegation, whether the agent returns a draft, a plan, or a full implementation, whether the human accepts or revises that output, and where the work re-enters ordinary team workflow. The HULA results show that HAI work often has a funnel structure: plan generation, human approval, code generation, human approval, pull request, standard team review. That funnel is precisely where TMS-like coordination routines become visible. ţłÇciteţłéturn23search0ţłéturn23search1ţłü

Fifth, it would need **time-varying context variables** that classical TMS studies usually take for granted: team familiarity, task interdependence, communication medium, AI literacy, model/version changes, interface redesigns, tool access asymmetries, and prompt or context-history persistence. In HAI, these are not nuisance variables. They are part of the construct environment because they shape whether the AI remains a stable memory partner or becomes a moving target. ţłÇciteţłéturn26search4ţłéturn29search0ţłéturn16search3ţłéturn34view0ţłü

Sixth, it would need **multiple outcomes** rather than one productivity metric: speed, defect rates, innovation or hypothesis generation, speaking up, workload, learning gains or skill atrophy, and team viability. The direct HAI evidence suggests that AI can help idea generation and voice in some settings, while coding studies show that speedups and slowdowns coexist depending on team, task, and familiarity. A credible panel should therefore expect heterogeneous effects rather than a single monotonic productivity answer. ţłÇciteţłéturn4search1ţłéturn11search5ţłéturn15search3ţłü

Operationally, the strongest design would combine a **baseline wave**, **monthly pulse surveys**, **continuous behavioral trace collection**, **quarterly deep-dive qualitative interviews**, and **event-triggered waves** after major shocks such as a model upgrade, a change in tool access, or a membership change. The fieldÔÇÖs newest meta-analytic evidence on measurement makes this triangulation especially important: self-report alone likely inflates apparent TMS effects relative to observer and embedded measures. ţłÇciteţłéturn28view0ţłéturn31view0ţłü

## Open questions and limitations

The biggest limitation is simply the state of the literature. Direct TMS measurement in human-AI teams remains minimal. Several newer HAI papers are important conceptually, but many are qualitative, perception-based, or adjacent rather than full construct validations. For a few studies, participant counts or exact durations were not recoverable from the accessible abstract or excerpt, so they are marked NR rather than inferred. The central gap is therefore not another short-term lab vignette. It is a sustained measurement program that can tell whether HAI teams actually develop stable, calibrated, longitudinal transactive memory systems rather than temporarily convenient patterns of AI use. ţłÇciteţłéturn4search1ţłéturn34view0ţłéturn28view0ţłü
